Push-ups

Difficulty
Intermediate
Time
10 minutes
Target Muscles
Chest, Shoulders, Triceps
Equipment
None

Description

Push-ups are a classic bodyweight exercise that primarily targets the chest, shoulders, and triceps while also engaging the core and other stabilizing muscles. They're an excellent compound movement for building upper body strength and can be modified to suit different fitness levels.

How to Perform

  1. Start in a plank position with your hands slightly wider than shoulder-width apart, arms straight, and body forming a straight line from head to heels.
  2. Keep your core engaged and glutes tight to maintain proper alignment.
  3. Lower your body by bending your elbows until your chest nearly touches the floor. Keep your elbows at about a 45-degree angle to your body.
  4. Push through your palms to extend your arms and return to the starting position.
  5. Maintain a straight body throughout the movement, avoiding sagging hips or arched back.
  6. Repeat for the desired number of repetitions, aiming for 3 sets of 10-15 reps with proper form.

Modifications

  • Beginner: Perform push-ups with knees on the ground to reduce resistance.
  • Intermediate: Standard push-ups as described above.
  • Advanced: Elevate your feet on a bench or perform clapping push-ups for increased difficulty.

Tips

  • Keep your body in a straight line from head to heels throughout the movement.
  • Focus on quality over quantity—perfect form is more important than high repetitions.
  • Breathe out as you push up, breathe in as you lower down.
  • If you experience wrist discomfort, try using push-up handles or perform the exercise on your knuckles.

Interval Running

Difficulty
Moderate
Time
20 minutes
Target Muscles
Legs, Cardiovascular System
Equipment
Running Shoes

Description

Interval running is a high-intensity cardio workout that alternates between periods of intense running and recovery periods of jogging or walking. This training method helps improve cardiovascular fitness, burn calories efficiently, and increase endurance.

How to Perform

  1. Begin with a 5-minute warm-up of light jogging or brisk walking.
  2. Sprint at 80-90% of your maximum effort for 30 seconds.
  3. Recover with a slow jog or walk for 60-90 seconds.
  4. Repeat this sprint-recovery cycle 8-10 times.
  5. Finish with a 5-minute cool-down of easy jogging and stretching.

Modifications

  • Beginner: Start with shorter sprint intervals (15-20 seconds) and longer recovery periods (2 minutes).
  • Intermediate: 30-second sprints with 60-90 second recovery as described above.
  • Advanced: Increase sprint duration to 45-60 seconds with shorter recovery periods (45 seconds).

Tips

  • Focus on proper running form with a slight forward lean and relaxed shoulders.
  • Land midfoot rather than on your heels to reduce impact.
  • Stay hydrated before, during, and after your workout.
  • Perform on varied terrain for additional challenge and to engage different muscles.

Yoga Flow

Difficulty
Beginner
Time
30 minutes
Target Muscles
Full Body, Core
Equipment
Yoga Mat

Description

Yoga Flow, also known as Vinyasa Yoga, is a practice that connects breath with movement through a series of poses. This flowing style of yoga improves flexibility, strength, and mental focus while reducing stress and promoting mindfulness.

How to Perform

  1. Begin in a comfortable seated position with eyes closed, focusing on deep breathing for 2-3 minutes.
  2. Move into a series of Sun Salutations, synchronizing breath with each movement.
  3. Flow through standing poses such as Warrior I, Warrior II, and Triangle Pose, holding each for 3-5 breaths.
  4. Transition to floor poses including seated forward folds and gentle twists.
  5. End with 5 minutes in Savasana (Corpse Pose) for deep relaxation.

Modifications

  • Beginner: Use props like blocks and straps to support proper alignment.
  • Intermediate: Deepen poses and extend hold times to 5-8 breaths.
  • Advanced: Add challenging variations and transitions between poses.

Tips

  • Focus on your breath throughout the practice, using it as an anchor for your attention.
  • Move at your own pace and respect your body's limitations.
  • Practice on an empty stomach, ideally 2-3 hours after eating.
  • Consistency is key—aim for regular practice rather than occasional intense sessions.

Squats

Difficulty
Intermediate
Time
15 minutes
Target Muscles
Quadriceps, Hamstrings, Glutes
Equipment
None (Bodyweight)

Description

Squats are a fundamental lower body exercise that targets multiple muscle groups simultaneously. This compound movement improves strength, mobility, and functional fitness while also engaging the core for stability.

How to Perform

  1. Stand with feet shoulder-width apart, toes slightly turned out.
  2. Keep your chest up and core engaged, with arms extended forward for balance.
  3. Bend your knees and push your hips back as if sitting in a chair.
  4. Lower until your thighs are parallel to the ground (or as low as comfortable with proper form).
  5. Drive through your heels to return to the starting position.
  6. Repeat for 3 sets of 12-15 repetitions.

Modifications

  • Beginner: Perform partial squats or use a chair for support.
  • Intermediate: Full bodyweight squats as described above.
  • Advanced: Add resistance with dumbbells, a barbell, or try single-leg variations.

Tips

  • Keep your weight in your heels and mid-foot, not your toes.
  • Ensure your knees track in line with your toes, not caving inward.
  • Maintain a neutral spine throughout the movement.
  • Focus on depth with proper form rather than quantity of repetitions.

Jumping Jacks

Difficulty
Beginner
Time
10 minutes
Target Muscles
Full Body, Cardiovascular
Equipment
None

Description

Jumping jacks are a classic cardio exercise that elevates your heart rate while engaging multiple muscle groups. This simple yet effective movement improves coordination, increases circulation, and serves as an excellent warm-up or cardio interval.

How to Perform

  1. Stand with feet together and arms at your sides.
  2. Jump while spreading your legs wider than shoulder-width apart.
  3. Simultaneously raise your arms above your head, ideally touching hands at the top.
  4. Jump again to return to the starting position, bringing your feet together and arms back to your sides.
  5. Repeat at a steady pace for 30-60 seconds per set.

Modifications

  • Beginner: Perform at a slower pace or step out to the sides instead of jumping.
  • Intermediate: Standard jumping jacks at a moderate to fast pace.
  • Advanced: Try plyo jacks (adding a deeper squat) or cross jacks (crossing arms and legs).

Tips

  • Land softly with slightly bent knees to reduce impact.
  • Maintain a consistent rhythm and controlled breathing.
  • Keep your core engaged throughout the exercise.
  • For longer sessions, break into intervals (30 seconds on, 10 seconds rest).

Balance Board Training

Difficulty
Advanced
Time
15 minutes
Target Muscles
Core, Ankles, Stabilizers
Equipment
Balance Board

Description

Balance board training challenges your stability and proprioception by requiring you to maintain balance on an unstable surface. This advanced exercise strengthens small stabilizing muscles, improves coordination, and enhances overall athletic performance.

How to Perform

  1. Place the balance board on a non-slip surface with enough space around you.
  2. Carefully step onto the board with feet shoulder-width apart.
  3. Find your center of gravity and attempt to keep the board level for 30-60 seconds.
  4. Progress to gentle tilting in different directions while maintaining control.
  5. For added challenge, try simple movements like squats or arm raises while balancing.

Modifications

  • Beginner: Start with a less challenging balance tool like a foam pad, or use a wall/support for assistance.
  • Intermediate: Practice basic balancing with occasional movements.
  • Advanced: Perform exercises like squats, single-leg stands, or even push-ups on the balance board.

Tips

  • Focus your gaze on a fixed point to help maintain balance.
  • Keep your core engaged throughout the exercise.
  • Start with shorter sessions and gradually increase duration as you improve.
  • Always prioritize safety—practice near a wall or sturdy object for support if needed.
